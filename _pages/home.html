Aloha! I am Raymond Wang, currently a Research Scientist at ByteDance AI Lab. Thank you for visiting my website! If you find my research or background interesting, please do not hesitate to connect with me. I look forward to getting to know you and sharing some exciting research.

I received my Ph.D. in Chemistry from Northwestern University in 2021. I was supervised by Dr. James Rondinelli, Dr. Kenneth Peoppelmeire, and Dr. Toru Shiozaki. My journey as a computational chemist started in 2014 when I bought my first MacBook and started learning programming. I was fascinated by theoretical modelling and computational simulation of quantum molecular systems, thanks to proper guidance from Dr. Zhipan Liu at Fudan University and Dr. Anastassia Alexandrova at UCLA.

I am particularly interested in interdisciplinary research interfacing drug discovery, quantum chemistry, materials science, optimization theory, as well as machine learning. Please see below for an overview of my research topics.

AI-Accelerated Drug Design & Discovery


Schematic protein-drug interaction model with learnable quantum mechanical and
molecular mechanical components. Our research objective is to efficiently
design candidate drug molecules exhibiting desirable properties.

I joined ByteDance in 07/2021 as a research scientist to focus on decoding the structure-activity relationship for biomolecular systems, e.g., enzymes, drug molecules, peptides, etc. With my previous research experience in quantum chemistry and materials physics, my goal is to develop fundamental theoretical framework for data-driven methods (i.e., AI models), which could be applied to solve existing challenges. I belive that physics-guided statistical models will become a significant complimentary method to the well-developed analytical/numerical solvers. The detailed research advances will be shared with the research community once we make significant breakthroughs. For more details please visit the Drug Discovery page. Please feel free to reach out to me for discussions and collaborations.

Quantum Chemistry


(a) Partition the active orbitals of benzene molecule into fragments and
(b) reconstruct the total wave function by iterative refinement.

In a nutshell, as a theoretical chemist, I develop quantitative numerical models to simulate and understand quantum molecular systems by solving the time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation. My research was mainly focused on developing active space decomposition (ASD) algorithm with second-order orbital optimization, and Douglas-Kroll-Hess (DKH) scalar relativistic approximation method. I contributed to the open-source quantum chemistry simulation package BAGEL, currently maintained by Dr. Toru Shiozaki and his deep tech startup QSimulate. I find the complex theoretical framework and computational modelling techniques extremely exciting, which was the main driving force for me to attend Northwestern for graduate studies. For more details on my research in this domain, please visit the Quantum Chemistry page, or connect with me to have a chat!

Materials Science & Engineering


Data-driven electronic materials design and discovery roadmap. By leveraging first principles
simulations, statistical learning models, and adaptive optimization algorithms, we achieve
a more cost-effective design framework for novel electronic materials.

During my Ph.D. studies, I developed a data-driven materials design framework by threading multiple research subdomains, i.e., first principles density functional theory (DFT) simulations, materials informatics, and optimization theory. Electronic materials research is intimately connected to condensed matter physics and quantum chemistry, with more focus on inorganic crystals with periodic boundary conditions. The materials systems under investigation have high degrees of freedom in terms of chemical compositions, microscopic lattice structures, as well as electronic/magnetic interactions. I start to realize that we may never have a universal solver to achieve "one model to solve them all". Therefore, interdisciplinary research is worth more attention owing to its power in bringing scientists and engineers with various backgrounds together to solve existing challenges. I enjoy learning new concepts and research methodologies from other domain experts. For more details on my work in this realm, please visit the Materials Science page.
